# Moral Money Community System – Operational Model

## 1. Core Ethical Principle

The system is built around a single guiding rule:

Power and reputation come from real contributions to the survival and prosperity of the community.

Contribution can take different forms:
- Work
- Capital
- Knowledge
- Infrastructure
- Services

No form of contribution is inherently superior. What matters is the real impact on the system.

---

## 2. System Architecture

The ecosystem has three main layers.

### Layer 1 – Blockchain (Protocol Layer)

The blockchain is the source of truth.

It records:
- identities
- projects
- participation
- outcomes
- reputation
- governance decisions

All critical decisions and state changes happen inside the protocol.

AI agents cannot override the protocol.

---

### Layer 2 – AI Domain Agents

Specialized AI agents analyze contributions within specific domains.

Examples:
- agriculture
- construction
- housing
- manufacturing
- energy
- health
- logistics

Agents analyze project data and provide structured evaluations.

They help interpret information but do not hold authority.

---

### Layer 3 – Community Interaction

Members interact with the system mainly through a mobile interface.

Users can:
- create projects
- join projects
- confirm participation
- close projects

The system must remain simple for participants.

Complexity exists in the algorithm, not in user actions.

---

## 3. Projects as the Basic Unit

All activity inside the community is structured as projects.

Projects can be large or small.

Examples:
- building houses
- farming fields
- installing infrastructure
- repairing equipment
- helping paint a room

Each project contains:
- creator
- participants
- domain
- expected outcome

---

## 4. Project Lifecycle

1. Project creation  
2. Participants join  
3. Work execution  
4. Project closure  
5. System evaluation  

Participants confirm completion through their devices.

---

## 5. Evaluation Model

Constant rating of people is avoided to preserve freedom and social trust.

Evaluation only happens when necessary.

Three mechanisms exist.

### Normal completion

If all participants confirm completion, the project closes automatically.

Contribution is recorded without detailed evaluation.

### Structured feedback

If feedback is required, a short evaluation form can be used.

Possible dimensions include:

- effort
- quality
- reliability
- collaboration
- impact

Each dimension can be rated on a five-star scale.

### Conflict resolution

If participants disagree strongly, the case is escalated.

The system selects five people with the highest reputation in that domain to review the case.

---

## 6. Reputation System

Reputation is not global.

It is domain specific.

Examples:
- construction reputation
- agriculture reputation
- governance reputation

Reputation grows through:

- repeated successful participation
- high-impact projects
- technical competence

Reputation decreases when:

- contributions repeatedly cause problems
- projects require significant rework

---

## 7. Governance Model

There is no permanent political class.

Decision groups are dynamic.

When a decision is needed in a domain:

The system automatically selects the five individuals with the highest relevant reputation.

Those five form a temporary decision council.

After the decision, the council dissolves.

---

## 8. Anti-Manipulation Logic

The algorithm must detect and reduce manipulation risks.

Key strategies:

- weight evaluations based on evaluator reputation
- detect rating patterns between specific users
- reduce influence of suspicious clusters
- require stronger evidence for large impacts

---

## 9. Algorithmic Complexity Principle

Users experience simplicity.

They only:

- create
- join
- complete

All fairness mechanisms exist inside the algorithm.

The system must absorb complexity so that community life remains natural.

---

## 10. Design Goal

Create a decentralized community where:

- authority emerges from contribution
- governance is merit-based
- manipulation is difficult
- freedom between people is preserved

---

## 11. Community Bootstrapping (Small Community Phase)

The system must work even when the community is very small.

During early stages the algorithm cannot rely on large data histories.

The first members create projects and record participation exactly as in the mature system.

However reputation grows slowly and requires repeated participation across different projects.

If the system cannot find five highly reputable members in a domain, it selects the most relevant available members.

Example:

- if only three qualified members exist, those three form the council  
- if only two exist, the system expands the pool to related domains  

The goal is to allow decisions without blocking the system.

---

## 12. Long-Term Objective

The system aims to support decentralized communities where:

- authority emerges from real contribution
- governance adapts dynamically to competence
- economic exchange remains transparent
- manipulation becomes increasingly difficult

The algorithm carries the complexity so that daily life inside the community can remain simple, free and cooperative.
